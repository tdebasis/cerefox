# Knowledge Management

Effective knowledge management is the practice of capturing, organising, and
retrieving information so that it remains useful over time. Unlike raw note-taking,
a mature knowledge management system connects ideas and surfaces them at the
right moment.

## The Second Brain Concept

The "second brain" metaphor, popularised by Tiago Forte, describes an external
system that supplements biological memory. The core idea is that your mind is
for generating ideas, not for storing them. Offloading storage to a trusted
external system frees cognitive capacity for deeper thinking.

A second brain typically involves four stages: capture, organise, distil, express
(the CODE framework). Capture means collecting anything that resonates. Organise
means sorting by actionability, not by topic. Distil means highlighting the most
valuable insights. Express means creating something new from what you have stored.

## Zettelkasten

Zettelkasten (German for "slip box") is a note-taking method developed by the
sociologist Niklas Luhmann, who used it to write over 70 books and hundreds of
articles. The method relies on three types of notes:

- **Fleeting notes**: quick captures, processed and discarded within a day or two
- **Literature notes**: distilled insights from a specific source
- **Permanent notes**: atomic, stand-alone ideas written in your own words

Each permanent note is linked to related notes by explicit references. Over time
the network of links creates emergent structure that reveals unexpected connections.

## Digital Tools

Modern knowledge management tools include Obsidian, Roam Research, Logseq, and
Notion. Each makes different trade-offs between structure and flexibility.

Obsidian stores notes as plain markdown files on disk — you own the data and can
use any other tool alongside it. Roam and Logseq use a block-based outliner model
that makes bidirectional linking and daily journaling natural. Notion combines
databases, pages, and rich media in a more structured product.

## Retrieval and Search

A knowledge base is only as useful as your ability to retrieve from it. Full-text
search is fast and exact — good for remembering a specific term you once used.
Semantic search finds related ideas even when the exact words differ. Combining
both approaches (hybrid search) gives the best coverage for personal knowledge bases.

## The Forgetting Curve and Spaced Repetition

Hermann Ebbinghaus showed that memory decays exponentially without reinforcement.
Spaced repetition systems (SRS) like Anki schedule reviews at expanding intervals
to fight this decay. Some knowledge management practitioners integrate SRS with
their note-taking so important ideas are reviewed before they fade.
