# Espresso Guide

Espresso is a concentrated coffee beverage brewed by forcing hot water under
pressure through finely ground coffee. A well-pulled shot is the foundation of
most café drinks: cappuccinos, flat whites, lattes, and Americanos all begin
with espresso.

## The Basics of Extraction

Extraction is the process of dissolving soluble compounds from ground coffee into
water. Under-extracted coffee tastes sour and thin; over-extracted coffee tastes
bitter and dry. The goal is a balanced extraction that captures sweetness,
acidity, and body in the correct proportions.

The key variables are:

- **Dose**: grams of ground coffee in the portafilter (typically 18–20 g)
- **Yield**: grams of liquid espresso in the cup (typically 36–40 g for a double)
- **Brew ratio**: yield divided by dose; 1:2 is a common starting point
- **Time**: the shot should pull in 25–35 seconds
- **Temperature**: 90–96 °C depending on the roast level
- **Pressure**: 9 bar is the espresso standard

## Grind Size and Its Effect

Grind size is the most powerful variable in espresso. Finer grinding increases
surface area, which speeds up extraction and increases resistance to water flow.
Coarser grinding reduces resistance and slows extraction.

If a shot pulls too fast (under 20 seconds) and tastes sour, grind finer.
If it pulls too slow (over 40 seconds) and tastes bitter, grind coarser.
Small adjustments — half a step on a quality burr grinder — make a significant
difference. Blade grinders produce uneven particle sizes and are unsuitable for
espresso.

## Milk Texturing

Steamed milk for espresso drinks should be smooth and glossy, with microfoam
integrated throughout rather than sitting on top as a stiff cap.

The technique involves submerging the steam wand just below the surface and
introducing a small vortex. In the first few seconds, draw in air (this is the
stretching phase). Then submerge the wand deeper to heat the milk without adding
more foam. Stop at 60–65 °C — above 70 °C the milk proteins denature and the
sweetness is lost.

## Single Origin vs Espresso Blends

Traditional espresso blends combine beans from multiple origins to achieve
balance: one component may provide body, another brightness, another sweetness.
Blending also reduces shot-to-shot variability.

Single-origin espresso uses beans from one farm or region. The flavour can be
striking — floral, fruity, or complex in ways a blend rarely achieves — but it
is less forgiving and requires precise dialling in. Light-roasted single origins
typically need higher temperatures (94–96 °C) and longer ratios (1:2.5 or more).

## Equipment Tiers

Entry-level machines (Breville Bambino, De'Longhi Dedica) are suitable for
learning but limit control. Mid-range machines (Breville Barista Express, Lelit
Mara) offer more stability and programmability. Prosumer machines (Rocket Appartamento,
ECM Synchronika) provide near-commercial performance at home.

A quality grinder matters more than the machine. A mediocre machine with a good
grinder will outperform an expensive machine paired with a poor grinder.
