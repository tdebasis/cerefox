# Ancient Rome

Rome grew from a small city-state on the Tiber River into one of the largest
empires in history, controlling territory from Britain to Mesopotamia at its
peak. Its influence on law, language, architecture, and governance continues
to shape the modern world.

## The Roman Republic

The Republic was established around 509 BC after the Romans expelled their
Etruscan kings. Power was distributed between two annually elected consuls,
the Senate, and various popular assemblies. The Senate was composed of former
magistrates and held enormous influence over foreign policy and finance.

The twelve tablets (451–450 BC) codified Roman law for the first time, making
legal principles accessible to ordinary citizens rather than subject to the
interpretation of patrician priests.

## The Late Republic and Its Crisis

By the first century BC the Republic was under severe strain. Military
commanders raised personal armies loyal to them rather than to the state.
Generals like Marius, Sulla, Pompey, and Julius Caesar used these armies
to accumulate political power.

Julius Caesar crossed the Rubicon river in 49 BC, an act of war against the
Senate, and within four years had made himself dictator perpetuo. His
assassination on the Ides of March, 44 BC, did not restore the Republic — it
triggered a series of civil wars that ended with his adopted heir Octavian
defeating Mark Antony at the Battle of Actium in 31 BC.

## The Principate

Octavian, renamed Augustus by the Senate, became the first emperor. He was
careful to preserve the forms of the Republic — the Senate continued to meet,
elections continued to be held — while holding real power through control of
the armies and the provinces.

Augustus's reign (27 BC – 14 AD) was marked by the Pax Romana, a long period
of relative peace and prosperity. He rebuilt Rome in marble, reorganised the
army and tax collection, and established a professional civil service.

## Roman Engineering and Architecture

Roman engineers developed concrete (opus caementicium) that could set underwater,
enabling the construction of harbours, bridges, and massive domed structures.
The Pantheon, completed under Hadrian around 125 AD, has the largest unreinforced
concrete dome in history and its proportions remain a reference point in
architectural education.

The Roman road network stretched over 400,000 km at its peak. Roads were built
to a standard design: a foundation of large stones, a layer of gravel and sand,
and a surface of polygonal basalt slabs. Many Roman roads remained in use into
the medieval period.

## The Fall of the Western Empire

Historians debate the causes of Rome's decline. Contributing factors include
military overextension, economic stagnation, political instability (the Crisis
of the Third Century saw over fifty claimants to the imperial throne in fifty
years), the increasing reliance on mercenary troops, and pressure from migrating
peoples along the frontiers.

The conventional date for the fall of the Western Roman Empire is 476 AD, when
the Germanic chieftain Odoacer deposed the last emperor, Romulus Augustulus.
The Eastern Empire, centred on Constantinople, survived as the Byzantine Empire
until 1453 AD.
